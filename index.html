Talia M. O'Shea

NSF Graduate Research Fellow in Astronomy at the University of Wisconsin-Madison working with Sebastian Heinz. Previously, I received my undergraduate degree in Astrophysics (and History!) from Wellesley College.

I am a computational astronomer broadly interested in the co-evolution of galaxies and their central supermassive black holes. I am also passionate about mentorship and education (see my CV for more details.)

Currently researching: radio-mode feedback via the propagation of relativistic AGN jets into their host galaxies.

Stellar mass-loading in AGN jets

AGN jets must be decelerated in order to deposit kinetic energy into their host galaxies. I investigate the process of stellar mass-loading as a source of deceleration, where stellar winds are swept up by the jet. The image on the left shows a snapshot from a simulation run in the moving-mesh code Arepo, where the jet moves from left to right over a star and mixes with the stellar wind material.

Dynamical friction in galaxy clusters

I tested the effect of dynamical friction on measurements of the splashback radius in galaxy clusters. There are discrepancies between splashback radii measured with dark matter and galaxy number density profiles, which are often attributed to dynamical friction. I ran simulations starting from galaxy cluster data from cosmological simulation IllustrisTNG, and found that dynamical friction minimally affects the measured splashback radius, meaning that there must be other contributing processes that are not yet understood!

Dynamical friction and measurements of the splashback radius in galaxy clusters, O'Shea T.M. et al., under review at OJAp.
